## The framework in brief

Yorke's central claim: every story follows a **5-act structure**, derived from Aristotle but made explicit. Not 3-act (setup / confrontation / resolution) — 5-act, because *development* and *crisis* are different animals that deserve their own acts.

**The 5 acts:**

1. **Setup** — status quo, then inciting incident
2. **Development** — "into the woods." The protagonist enters unfamiliar territory, learns new rules
3. **Crisis / Midpoint** — point of no return. Internal journey becomes external. Commitment.
4. **Climax** — worst moment. Everything collides.
5. **Resolution** — new equilibrium. Transformation revealed, or its refusal confirmed.

**The "into the woods" metaphor** (the book's title): every story is a journey from the known world into chaos, where the protagonist confronts their shadow and returns either changed or confirmed tragic.

**Other Yorke principles we will use:**

- **Fractal structure** — every act is itself 5-act; every scene has 5 beats
- **Internal vs external journey must mirror** — what the character *wants* (external) must echo what they *need* (internal)
- **Flaw / fault line** — the protagonist has a psychological flaw that drives the plot; by the end it's either healed or confirmed tragic
- **Antagonist as mirror** — the opposition character embodies what the protagonist fears or needs to become. Not a villain.
- **Beats must rhyme** — in a braided structure, the acts of both arcs should echo each other

## The beats must rhyme

This is the craft insight Yorke enables. Each act of Istanbul should echo the parallel act in the 5-year arc:

- Istanbul arrival ↔ first meeting (the hope, the before)
- Istanbul walks ↔ relationship development (intimacy returning, or its ghost)
- Istanbul pivot ↔ Glasgow move (a choice point)
- Istanbul breakup ↔ scholarship betrayal (the wound repeats with variation)
- Istanbul departure ↔ retreat to Russia (parting — but different this time)

When the reader is in Istanbul Act 3 and you flashback to Glasgow Act 3, they should feel the rhyme *without you having to explain it*. That's how braided structure generates meaning.

## Character arcs — revelation, not transformation

Remember: in *Before Sunset* nobody changes. The arc is **revelation**. Yorke's framework accommodates this via the failed-transformation ending (which he treats as its own legitimate outcome, not a failure of craft).

### You (narrator / protagonist)

**External want:** her back, proof she loved you, a clean ending in Istanbul.

**Internal need** *(early guess — to test against chat evidence)*: to see her as a separate person, not a projection. To stop waiting. To release the story without releasing the love.

**Flaw / fault line** *(speculative, flag for correction)*: you may love in a way that absorbs the other person. You moved cities for her. You waited 9 months. The scholarship betrayal devastates you beyond "she lied" — she carved out a self separate from you, and you may not have had a self to fall back on. This is an early-draft read, not a verdict.

**Arc possibilities for Istanbul:**

- **Revelation-with-release** — you see her clearly and can love her without being consumed → literary triumph, maybe too neat
- **Revelation-without-release** — you see her clearly and still can't let go → tragic, honest, *Before Sunset*–like
- **Denial** — you leave with the projection intact → flattens the book, dodges the material

The honest ending probably sits between #1 and #2. Ambiguous. We'll know when we get there.

### Her (opposition character, rendered through your POV)

**External want** *(as you perceive)*: scholarship, a future of her own, autonomy.

**Internal need** *(unknown — chat will show it)*. Possibilities:

- To not be consumed by your love (mirror of your flaw)
- To have something that's hers alone — the scholarship concealment as a distorted expression of that
- To manage guilt or shame, especially post-2022, loving a Russian during the war
- Something we don't yet have language for

**She is not the antagonist.** Yorke's "opposition character" isn't the villain — she's the force that exposes the protagonist's flaw by embodying what he can't be. Her *separateness* is what breaks you. That's the real opposition.
