## Structural aim of Act 1

Set up: the protagonist, the woman who upends him, the world they move through, and the pattern — his sacrifice, her refusal — that will repeat in variations across the rest of the book. End at the deletion: the first concrete act of self-erasure. By the last page of Act 1 the reader should feel two things at once: *this relationship is extraordinary* AND *it is already carrying the flaw that will end it*.

**Target length:** ~22,000–27,000 words. Nine chapters averaging ~2,500.

## Chapter 1 — May Dip  *(reshaped 2026-04-19)*

- **When:** Apr 30 2019 evening → May 1 2019 dawn.
- **Where:** Narrator's house (5 Windmill Road) → girls' dorm → East Sands → home.
- **POV:** First person. Past tense. Retrospective voice implicit — narrator in 2026.
- **What changes:** Before this chapter he is alone in his Sergeyeva-grief. After it, another shape has entered the grief. He does not yet know it.
- **Word target:** ~3,000–3,500.

### Seven-beat structure

**Beat 1 — 5 Windmill Road, before Mikael.** (~500–700 words)
Friends at the house. Weed, beer, music. Narrator on aux. Ordinary Scottish evening. One small moment that plants the grief-weight without naming it. Ends at the threshold — Mikael's arrival imminent.

**Beat 2 — Mikael's "schoolgirls."** (~200–300 words)
Arrival. The "школьницы" line. Foundation-programme joke. Narrator half-checks, doesn't rise to it.

**Beat 3 — The three girls arrive.** (~500–700 words)
Lera, Anna-Maria, Sasha. Tom charming. Narrator on music. One thing about Lera registers *despite* his unavailability — small, unplaced.

**Beat 4 — Walk to the dorm.** (~300–400 words)
Night air, St Andrews streets. Girls' dorm — concrete details.

**Beat 5 — Dorm → seaside. Fragments.** (~400–600 words)
Drinking harder. Moving toward the sea. Prose becomes fragmentary — drunkenness in the sentences, not the explanation.

**Beat 6 — What Lera told him later.** (~200–400 words)
The unreliable-narrator moment. *Lera told me, months later, I made passes at her that night. I don't remember making them.* Establishes on page 1 that the narrator does not reliably witness his own life.

**Beat 7 — Home. Falling asleep.** (~300–400 words)
Walking back. Sky going grey. His bed. Unbidden: her. Falls asleep thinking of her without understanding why. Something moved in him that hasn't moved in a year. Theme born, unnamed.

### Key craft rules

1. Never say *little did I know.*
2. Never say *I wasn't interested in her.* Show by writing him not looking.
3. Three girls, one gets weight — but only in retrospect.
4. Sergeyeva stays unnamed in Ch 1. First naming is Lera's in 2022 corpus.
5. Don't let the grief become explicit subject matter. Grief is the *air* of the chapter, not a soloist.
6. One line of dialogue for Lera. Small. Ordinary. She is a girl before she is mythic.
7. The Lera-told-me-later moment must land. It is the unreliability engine for the whole book.

### Decisions needed before drafting Beat 1

1. Narrator's fictional name (placeholder OK).
2. Which room of 5 Windmill Road.
3. Who else is in the house besides Tom (1–2 more, named with one quality each).
4. The music — specific album/song or just ambient.
5. The ordinary conversation topic in Beat 1.
6. The texture moment — which gesture renders the grief.
7. The threshold — text, knock, keys.
8. Date/time — April 30 2019, what hour does Beat 1 open.

## Chapter 2 — The First Year

- **When:** Summer 2019 → spring 2020.
- **Where:** St Andrews (her), London / elsewhere (him), everywhere via Telegram.
- **Scenes:** Montage + one or two specific set pieces. Constant communication. Early intimacy. The unspoken question of what they are to each other.
- **What changes:** They become what they become. No clean moment — gradient.
- **Craft:** This chapter has no textual evidence (deleted July 2020). Lean into that — it reads as memory, softer-edged, impressionistic. The contrast with later chapters that read ground-truthed is the point. The narrator's uncertainty about what was real is on the page.
- **Word target:** ~3,000.

## Chapter 3 — The Drop-Out

- **When:** Late 2019 / early 2020.
- **Where:** London (the company).
- **Scene:** The conversation where he leaves the company he co-founded. Who he tells first — co-founders, parents, her. What he says. What he doesn't say.
- **What changes:** He gives up a concrete future. The company will go on without him and succeed.
- **Craft:** Let the narrator bundle "masters + her" as the reason in the moment — then let a later sentence or chapter quietly expose the bundling. Show the narrator writing his own myth. More honest than the myth being true.
- **Word target:** ~2,500.

## Chapter 4 — Glasgow

- **When:** Late 2019 or early 2020.
- **Where:** Glasgow, St Andrews.
- **Scenes:** Moving. The flat. The light in Glasgow vs the light in London. First train from Glasgow to St Andrews. **"I never asked you to do this" — first appearance of the refrain.**
- **What changes:** He rearranges his geography for her. She names it as his choice.
- **Word target:** ~3,000.

## Chapter 5 — The Commute

- **When:** 2020.
- **Where:** 12-hour bus. Glasgow ↔ London or similar.
- **Scene:** One specific journey, rendered in full.
- **What changes:** Nothing plot-wise. Everything atmospherically. The length of the bus is the measure. Show the sacrifice; don't explain it.
- **Craft:** Very quiet chapter. A stranger across the aisle. A service station at 3 a.m. The mileage signs. What he thought about to pass the hours. The narrator must not say *I did this for her* — the reader sees it.
- **Word target:** ~2,000.

## Chapter 6 — The Visa

- **When:** 2020, ~1.5 months.
- **Where:** His flat (London or Glasgow — user to confirm).
- **Scenes:** Cohabitation. Morning coffee. The walk to the shop. A stupid fight. Reconciliation. Sex, written plainly. The good version of the relationship.
- **What changes:** The reader sees what he has. Without this chapter, they will not understand what he loses.
- **Craft:** This is the chapter that earns the tragedy. The reader falls for her here. Specific details. What she cooked. What book she left open. What song she played twice.
- **Word target:** ~3,500.

## Chapter 7 — New Year Alone

- **When:** Dec 31 2019 → Jan 1 2020 (verify year with user).
- **Where:** His flat.
- **Scenes:** He asks her to stay. She refuses. He is alone. Midnight. Fireworks he doesn't go out to see.
- **What changes:** The first clean refusal. She chose something else over him.
- **Craft:** Short. Quiet. Devastating without drama. Possibly the shortest chapter in the book.
- **Word target:** ~1,500.

## Chapter 8 — The Grandfather

- **When:** Late June / early July 2020.
- **Where:** Moscow.
- **Scenes:** His grandfather is sick with covid. Narrator is scared and alone. He offers to pay for her tickets. Her parents refuse. She does not acknowledge the grandfather. The fight escalates over days. The physical-affection tension from prior months bleeds in as memory or subtext.
- **What changes:** The sacrifice / refusal pattern reaches breaking pressure. Something has to give.
- **Word target:** ~3,000.

## Chapter 9 — The Deletion

- **When:** July 2020.
- **Where:** Moscow.
- **Scenes:** The fight's aftermath. The decision. The mechanical act of deletion — opening Telegram, selecting the chat, pressing options, confirming. Two years erased in minutes.
- **What changes:** The archive is gone. The relationship survives. The record doesn't.
- **Craft:** Act 1 climax. Render the banality of destroying two years. Don't editorialize. Show the UI. Show his finger. Let the reader feel the casualness of the act and the enormity of what it erases.
- **Word target:** ~2,000.
